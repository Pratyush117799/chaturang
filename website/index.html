The Legend

चतुरंग — The Game of Four Divisions

Born in ancient India, this probabilistic war game commanded kings and scholars alike for over 1,500 years.

Ancient Origins

Chaturanga — Sanskrit for "four divisions" — represents the four branches of the Gupta Empire's military: Hastī (elephant), Ashva (horse), Ratha (chariot), and Padāti (infantry). First documented in the 6th century CE, it is the oldest known ancestor of modern Chess, Xiangqi, and Shogi.

The Uncertainty Element

Unlike Chess, Chaturanga includes a die (pāśaka) — you must move the piece whose number appears on the roll. A roll of 1 forces the Ratha, 3 forces the Ashwa, 4 forces the Danti, and 6 allows the Nara or Raja. This uncertainty of fate reflects ancient Indian philosophy of dharma and destiny.

Four Kingdoms at War

The classic form is a 4-player alliance game on the Ashtāpada (8×8 board). Red & Green form one alliance; Blue & Yellow another. You play for your team — if your king falls, your ally can revive you. The last alliance standing wins the Ashtāpada.

Distinct from Chess

There is no Queen — only the Raja (King) and his four forces. Minor pieces (Nara, Ashva, Hathi) cannot capture Major pieces (Ratha, Raja). A slain king can return — if your teammate captures an enemy king, your Raja respawns anywhere on the board. Stalemate means the one who stalemates wins.

Documentation & Research

Authentic historical sources, algorithmic designs, and the living academic record of Chaturanga.

~6th Century CE Primary Source

The Harshacharita Reference

Bāṇabhaṭṭa's biographical work on King Harsha (7th century CE) contains one of the earliest literary references to chaturanga as a game played by the nobility. The text describes the four-division formation as both a military reality and a game of intellectual strategy.

"The game reflects the order of the cosmos — four forces, four directions, one sovereign dharma."
~1030 CE Islamic Scholarship

Al-Bīrūnī — Kitāb al-Hind

Persian scholar Abū Rayhān Al-Bīrūnī documented Chaturanga in his monumental work on India. He noted similarities to chess and described the four-player variant with dice, confirming the ancient Indian origin of the game. His accounts are among the most reliable cross-cultural records.

"The Hindus have a game like chess for four persons, played according to the throw of dice. The pieces are elephant, horse, ship, and foot soldier..." — Al-Bīrūnī, Kitāb al-Hind (c. 1030 CE)
1913 Foundational Research

H.J.R. Murray — A History of Chess

Harold James Ruthven Murray's landmark study A History of Chess (1913, Oxford) remains a definitive academic reference. Murray traced the evolution from Chaturanga through Chatrang (Persia) to Shatranj (Arabic world) to modern chess, establishing the Indian origin theory as scholarly consensus.

Murray, H.J.R. (1913). A History of Chess. Oxford: Clarendon Press. Chapter II: The Indian Game.
Digital Age Project Research

Authentic Rules & Variant Analysis

The Chaturanga project founders conducted a comprehensive analysis of rule variants across historical sources — Maṅgalagiri version, Abul-fazl's version (Ain-i-Akbari), and regional South/North Indian variants. Key distinctions catalogued:

  • Elephant movement — 3 documented variants (2-diagonal, 2-orthogonal, or Thai-inspired)
  • Minor vs Major capture hierarchy (unique to this project's 1.0.3.2 rules)
  • King respawn mechanic — sourced from Maṅgalagiri MS
  • Dice assignment: 1=Ratha, 3=Ashwa, 4=Danti, 6=Nara/Raja

Founders & Researchers

Project Founders

The Chaturanga project was initiated by a team of researchers passionate about reviving ancient Indian board games. Their handwritten notes, rule analysis, and algorithm designs form the foundation of this digital implementation.

Bot Engine Team

The bot algorithm uses a probabilistic decision tree with minimax + alpha-beta pruning adapted for Chaturanga's dice uncertainty. The engine accounts for forced pieces, minor/major capture rules, and team alliance logic.

Probabilistic Decision Tree

Chaturanga is a probabilistic game — unlike deterministic Chess, the bot must reason about all possible dice outcomes before choosing a move. The engine builds a decision tree where each node represents a game state, and branches are weighted by dice probability.

Dice faces: 4 (1, 3, 4, 6) Branching factor: ~15-40 moves/state Search depth: 2-3 plies (configurable)

Random Move Generator (v1.0.3.2)

The current bot (v1.0.3) uses an enhanced random move generator — it picks legal moves for the forced piece, with preference scoring based on captures, board center control, and king safety. The engine respects minor/major capture constraints.

// Piece priority weights
const weights = {
  king:     { capture_major: 100, center: 5 },
  rook:     { capture_any:   80,  center: 8 },
  horse:    { capture_minor: 30,  center: 6 },
  elephant: { capture_minor: 20,  center: 4 },
  pawn:     { advance:       10,  capture: 15 }
};

Planned: Minimax + Alpha-Beta Pruning

The roadmap includes a full minimax implementation adapted for 4-player alliance games. Standard minimax assumes two adversaries; the alliance variant requires a coalition utility function that balances individual piece values against team win conditions.

Piece Movements

Raja (King)

One step in any direction (8-directional). Can be captured, but ally can revive it.

Ratha (Chariot)

Any number of squares along ranks or files — like the chess Rook. Cannot jump.

Ashva (Horse)

L-shape: 2 squares in one direction + 1 perpendicular. Jumps over pieces like the Knight.

Danti (Elephant)

Exactly 2 squares diagonally, leaping over any piece. 4 possible target squares.

Nara (Pawn)

One step forward. Captures diagonally. No double-move on first step. No en passant.

Key Rules (v1.0.3.2)

  • Minor cannot capture Major. Nara, Ashva, Danti cannot capture Ratha or Raja.
  • Major captures any. Ratha and Raja can capture any piece.
  • King Respawn. If your teammate captures an enemy Raja, your own Raja may be placed on any empty square.
  • Max 3 manual forfeits per player—then you must always use your forced piece.
  • Stalemate = Win. If you can't move your forced piece, you win (not lose as in Chess).
  • No castling, no en passant, no Queen.
